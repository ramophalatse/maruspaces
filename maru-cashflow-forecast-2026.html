Maru Spaces enters the forecast period in a position of material liquidity strength, with an opening cash balance of R5.69M, the result of the GDID settlement receipt and active financial stewardship over the preceding period.

On contracted work alone, the practice operates at a modest average monthly shortfall against its cost base. This is not a structural concern: architectural fees are stage-gated by design, with large milestone billings at fixed points across the project lifecycle. The appropriate measure is not month-by-month cash movement, but average monthly inflows against average monthly costs, a view that shows the business at or near operating breakeven on committed work.

With the R6.8M total pipeline (Bongolethu JPS and King Hintsa TVET College, inclusive of contracted billings) spread across 18 months, average monthly inflows reach R378k against an operating cost base of approximately R392k — a gap of under R15k per month, and one that closes entirely as further mandates are formalised. The R5.69M opening balance provides a meaningful timing buffer while we convert projects in the pipeline.

The forward position is sound. The priority is converting pipeline and ensuring provisional tax is modelled before the August P1 window. The forecast also incorporates a September dividend declaration of R148,000 to Maps, with R37,000 in Dividends Withholding Tax (DWT) remitted to SARS within 5 business days of declaration. A combined R185,000 outflow from Maru, reflected in the September figures.

The balance floor across the forecast period is R4.15M — a projected minimum inclusive of the September dividend payment (R148k) and DWT (R37k). Pipeline conversion will push the actual closing position materially higher.
Liquidity floor
R4.15M closing balance is built on contracted work only, and includes the September dividend payment of R148k and DWT of R37k. With R6.8M total pipeline not yet fully reflected, the actual floor is higher, this is a conservative base case by design.
Stage-gated billing pattern
Deficit months are not a sign of financial stress, they reflect the normal rhythm of an architectural practice. Income is milestone-driven; the cost base runs continuously between those events.
Items to model
Provisional tax (P1 August window) is unmodelled and should be estimated before the filing date. VAT outflows of R361k are scheduled and appear correctly timed against billing receipt months.

The averaged view normalises the distortion of stage-gated billing and reveals the underlying economics of the practice more clearly than any single month's cash movement.

On contracted work alone, Maru runs at an average shortfall of approximately R150k per month against its total cost base, but this figure includes R361k of VAT outflows across the period, which are tax settlements rather than operating costs. Stripping VAT, the operating gap narrows to roughly R110k per month on contracted work.

Against the full R6.8M pipeline spread across 18 months, average monthly inflows reach R378k. Against the operating cost base of R392k, the gap is under R15k per month, one that closes with any meaningful pipeline conversion.

The business is at or near operating breakeven on committed work. It is one additional mandate from moving into sustained operating surplus. The settlement-derived opening balance provides a timing buffer for that conversion, but should not be read as evidence of a historically strong cash position.
